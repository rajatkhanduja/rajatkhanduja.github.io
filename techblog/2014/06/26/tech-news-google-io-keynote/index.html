  1. Google Introduces Android One Program To Bring More Smartphones To Developing Markets

    • After sharing that Google now has 1 billion 30-day active users Sundar Pichai announced the Android One program which would help increase the number significantly.

    • This initiative will target OEMs, specifically in emerging markets, to make smartphones more affordable to develop and release.

      With Android One, Google is hoping to reduce the cost of smartphone development by identifying the components that go into a smartphone and to pre-qualify suppliers for those parts to ensure they work together.

      On the software side, Google is giving hardware manufacturers access to its Play Store to help them add locally relevant apps to their devices. It’s also handling all the software updates automatically, just like it does for Nexus devices.

    • Three OEMs to start with : Cromax, Karbonn and Spice.
    • Google is also trying to work with wireless providers to provide low cost wireless plans.
  2. Google Unveils New Cross Platform Design Language “Material Design”
    • As a part of the forthcoming, Android ‘L’ release of the Android OS, Google announced a new universal design language, “Material Design”.

      “We imagined… what if pixels didn’t just have color, but also depth? What if there was a material that could change its texture? This lead us to something we call ‘material design,” says Matias Durate, Director of Android operating system User Experience at Google…

    • The new framework is now publicly available.
    • They aim is to put the framework out in the public to help create a consistent user-experience for the different Android apps, much like Apple’s guidelines for Mac and iOS developers.
    • Redesigned versions of their flagship products like GMail and Calendar will soon be introduced (for both Android and web)

      Google describes the new design as being “inspired by the study of paper and ink, yet technologically advanced and open to imagination and magic.”

      The design uses familiar tactile means of interacting with its many parts, with visual cues that are grounded in reality, Google says. Its elements also recall print-based design typography, with “deliberate color choices, edge-to-edge imagery, large-scale typography, and intentional white space create a bold and graphic interface that immerses the user in the experience.”

      Motion is another key element of the design, but is meant to be. “Motion is meaningful and appropriate, serving to focus attention and maintain continuity,” Google adds.

    • Such a design centric update to Android was expected and several websites reported the internal project ‘Quantum’
    • This move seems to show Google’s increased focus on user-experience and design, which seems to have been a distinguishing factor between iOS and Android devices.
  3. Google Makes Phone Logins Easier With Personal Login Feature For Android ‘L’
    • Google demoed a feature that allows the user to skip logging into the phone as the phone deduces if it is in a trusted environment.
    • The phone looks for your smartphone or your Wifi network; if found, it doesn’t require you to authenticate yourself through a PIN or pattern.
    • In case it senses an unknown/untrusted environment, the PIN/pattern based login is used.
    • This seems to be aimed at competing with Apple’s built-in fingerprint scanner.
  4. Android Wear Smartwatches and SDK

    • After announcing that LG’s and Samsung’s first Android Wear Smartphone watches will go on sale immediately, Google announced that it will be releasing Android Wear SDK for developers to build upon.

      … Google demoed several Android Wear functions that will be available on all Android Wear devices including swiping to dismiss messages and displaying contextual information relevant to the wearer.

    • On stage, there was also an interesting demo of how the smartwatch showed contextually relevant information.
      • The wearer had a flight ticket for that day, which was displayed on the watch, like a Google Now card.
      • One could see more information by tapping on it.
      • An incoming message (on the phone) notification was shown on the watch and swiping it away also removed the notification from the phone.
      • It was also able to give directions to a place and log/display the number of steps taken by the wearer.
      • Also, it was able to respond to voice commands like searching, responding to a message and setting an alarm.

    • The SDK allows developers to create custom UI, allowing their apps to leverage the sensors, taps and voice commands on the smartwatches.

      The company demoed an app from Eat24, for example, which would allow you to order a pizza from the watch within 30 seconds and just a few taps.

    • Besides LG and Samsung, they have some other partners. Moto 360 is expected later this year. Other partners include Asus, Qualcomm, Intel and HTC.

    • Android Wear, for style, supports both square and round shaped watches. LG and Samsung’s first watches are square-shaped, while Moto360, will be round shaped.
  5. Google Introduces Android TV, Its New Platform For Smart TV Apps And Navigation
    • Referring to it as the ‘biggest screen in the house’, Google announced a new Android TV operating system.
    • It’s software (OS) that will be embedded into smart televisions by third party OEMs, much like the Android OS was used by smartphone manufacturers
    • Android TV will work with a number of chipsets and is designed to enable developers to create apps using the same Android toolset they used for phones and tablets.
    • Android TV will play movies and TV shows and could be controlled by phones, tablets and even smartwatches.
    • Your smartphone or tablet could act as the controller for games played on Android TV.
    • It will have full Chromecast support, which itself received significant updates, allowing users to beam content from their mobile devices to their TV.
    • This is not the first time Google is trying to enter the TV market. Earlier attempts, including Google TV, didn’t succeed.
  6. Google Announces Android Auto, Promises Enabled Cars By The End Of 2014
    • Lately, a lot of road accidents have been attributed to drivers fumbling with their phones. With the intention to change this and allow user to leverage the power of Android without losing sight of the road, Google announced Android Auto
    • Android-auto connects the user’s smartphone with the car, to provide context based information, navigation and notifications on a large-enough screen on the dashboard.
    • The user can interact with it using voice commands or keys on the steering. While the touch based navigation still works, it’s not as bad as dealing with the small screen of the phone.
    • During the demo, they showed how a driver could start navigation to a destination through just voice commands.
    • Integration with Google Now allows Android-auto to read your text messages out loud to you while you are driving and allows you to dictate and send a reply.
    • 25 car manufacturers have promised to build Android-Auto powered cars and the first cars are expected to roll out by the end of this year.
    • Android Auto SDK will be released soon.
  7. Google Blurs The Line Between Web And Native Apps On Android
    • Google demoed the new version of Chrome for the next Android version (Android ‘L’).

      But it isn’t just a better version of Chrome. There is a clear and more profound message coming from Google. The company wants to blend native apps with web tabs. And the end of native apps as we know them could be closer than we might think.

    • App switcher now looks like Safari on iOS, with recent apps looking like cards in a drawer.
    • Each tab in Chrome gets is shown like a separate app!

    • Google has already been supporting deep-links to other applications through search results. For instance, if you searched for a book on Google Now, the search result that links to Goodreads, could actually launched the Goodreads app, letting you leverage the interface designed by Goodreads for mobile. They are now expanding this feature to many more apps.

      Knowing all this, imagine for a second what Android L will look like. You turn on your phone, search for something in Google using voice search on your home screen. It launches Chrome. You tap on the first search result, it launches a native app. You switch apps to read this article you found earlier in Chrome.

      Back, and forth, and back, and forth between the web and native apps. After a while, you won’t even notice if you’re on the web or in a native app.

    • Google is possibly doing this because it has been traditionally centered around web with most of its revenue coming from ads.
    • It could be possible that in the future Google tries to make Android a very thin OS layer letting all the apps be web-based. This would be something like Mozilla’s FirefoxOS or Palm’s WebOS
  8. Google will soon bring Android apps to Chrome OS
    • The Android apps will essentially run in an emulator on the Chrome OS Desktop and will be available in the launcher.
    • No expected time was given for this feature, however, they did demo a few apps (eg: Evernote) to show how it would work.
    • They also seem to be looking at a tighter integration of Chrome OS and Android.
      • If you come close to your Chromebook with your phone, it will automatically log you in, skipping the authentication step.
      • This is quite like the smartwatch unlocking your Android phone.
      • Also, the notifications on your Android phone would be available on Chromebook as desktop notifications. Currently, this is possible to achieve using a combination of app on Android and extension on Google Chrome. PushBullet, too, provides this feature.
  9. Chromecast Update Introduces An AirPlay-Like Android Mirroring Feature, Photo Backdrops And More
    • Chromecast, Google’s popular $35 dongle that plugs into your TV’s HDMI port, letting you stream video and music to your television, received a number of updates.

    • A new feature allows anyone to share the screen without the hassle of logging onto the same wifi network as the Chromecast enabled television.

      “So how do we do that?,” asks Chandra. “We’re using a variety of technologies to confirm that you’re in the same room as the Chromecast. If for some reason we can’t automatically detect that, you can enter an on-screen PIN,” he explains.

    • This would make it more convenient for some guest to share photos or videos on the TV.
    • Another feature, Backdrop, lets you turn ‘the biggest screen’ in the house into a photo frame of sorts when it is not being used otherwise.
    • It lets you slide through your own photo albums (on Google+) or a random set of images showing scenic landscapes, art, lifestyle, etc.

    • The Chromecast app on your mobile could be used to learn more about the images on screen (in case you like the image or want to know more about it).

      However, the standout feature in the Chromecast update is its new ability to allow any Android device to be mirrored, wirelessly, onto a TV. Google built its own framework for this, allowing it to cut down on the latency, Chandra said.
      During the demo, the company showed how you can switch this feature on from the settings, making everything on your phone immediately appear on the TV, including things like Google Earth and even your phone’s camera viewfinder – the latter which was aimed at the audience for a little frivolity before wrapping up the announcement.

  10. Updates to Google Cloud Platform: Data Flow, Cloud monitoring and Cloud Save

    • Google announced a new addition to its Cloud Platform, Data Flow

      Cloud Dataflow is a fully managed service for creating data pipelines that ingest, transform and analyze data in both batch and streaming modes. Cloud Dataflow is a successor to MapReduce, and is based on our internal technologies like Flume and MillWheel.

      Cloud Dataflow makes it easy for you to get actionable insights from your data while lowering operational costs without the hassles of deploying, maintaining or scaling infrastructure. You can use Cloud Dataflow for use cases like ETL, batch data processing and streaming analytics, and it will automatically optimize, deploy and manage the code and resources required.

    • Cloud montoring:
      • A monitoring tool to find and fix unusual behavior across your application stack.
      • It provides metrics, dashboards and alerting for Google Cloud Platform and several popular services like Apache, Nginx, MySQL, etc.
      • Google’s earlier acquisition of Stackdriver could have enabled them to integrate their monitoring tool to create this.
    • Cloud Trace and Cloud Debugger provide tools to debug and find performance bottlenecks in a live application.
    • In the demo at I/O, they set a watchpoint in a live application to to debug a ‘live call’.

    • Google Cloud Save provides a simple API to developers for saving, retrieving and synchronizing user data on the cloud and across devices without the need of any server code.

    • Most of these services are still in private beta and will be launched soon
  11. Google Fit

    • Google Fit will be a set of APIs that will allow developers to sync data across wearables and devices.

      The dream here is the bundling of user fitness data, with the user’s permission, of course. And Google is not alone in this aspiration, with Apple’s HealthKit platform among the many products aiming to aggregate your health zeros and ones.

    • The SDK will roll out in a couple of weeks
    • They already have partnered with brands to provide integration with Adidas Smart sensors and Nike Fuel.
